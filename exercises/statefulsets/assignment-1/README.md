# Assignment 1: StatefulSets

StatefulSets are the workload controller for applications that need three guarantees a Deployment cannot provide: stable pod network identity backed by predictable DNS names, ordered pod lifecycle (creation in order from `pod-0` upward and deletion in reverse), and per-pod persistent storage that survives rescheduling and scale-down. This is the only assignment in the StatefulSets series; it covers the complete CKA-relevant surface in one pass, including the StatefulSet spec, the headless Service pairing, `volumeClaimTemplates` mechanics, `podManagementPolicy`, `RollingUpdate` with `partition` and the v1.35 Beta `maxUnavailable` field, `OnDelete` strategy, scaling behavior, and the diagnostic workflow for stuck pods and blocked rollouts. By the end of the assignment you should be able to reach for a StatefulSet without hesitation whenever a workload needs sticky identity or sticky storage, and be able to diagnose a StatefulSet in trouble from its Events and ControllerRevision history.

## Recommended Workflow

Work through the tutorial in your cluster before starting the exercises. The tutorial builds one complete StatefulSet workflow with a headless Service, `volumeClaimTemplates`, and a partitioned rolling update, demonstrating the field defaults and failure modes as it goes so you can see them play out rather than read them from a reference card. Once the tutorial is finished, move through the 15 homework exercises in order. Levels 1 and 2 build construction fluency: a basic StatefulSet, per-pod storage, `Parallel` management, rolling updates with `partition`, and the `OnDelete` strategy. Level 3 is debugging with three silent-failure scenarios (StorageClass mismatch, non-headless Service, serviceName mismatch) that together cover the three most common ways a StatefulSet looks "applied cleanly" but does not deliver its guarantees. Level 4 covers multi-tier realistic scenarios including staged rollouts with `partition`. Level 5 includes one complex multi-tier design task, one advanced multi-bug debug scenario, and a staged-rollout task that exercises the full rolling-update mental model. Use the answer key only after a genuine attempt, and read the Diagnosis section first when tackling the debug solutions.

## Difficulty Progression

Level 1 covers core construction: a minimal StatefulSet plus headless Service, `volumeClaimTemplates` for per-pod storage, and the `Parallel` vs `OrderedReady` distinction. Level 2 combines two or three concepts per exercise: verifying storage persists across a pod restart, performing a partitioned rolling update, and exercising the `OnDelete` strategy. Level 3 is debugging with the anti-spoiler convention applied: exercise headings are bare, objectives state the desired end state, each exercise contains exactly one silently-failing problem. Level 4 builds realistic production patterns: a multi-tier application with a StatefulSet database and a Deployment frontend communicating through stable DNS, a staged partition-based rollout, and scale operations that preserve PVCs. Level 5 includes a clustered-application design task, a multi-bug advanced debug scenario, and a scripted staged rollout that mirrors real-world canary deployment workflows.

## Cluster Requirements

A multi-node kind cluster is the right environment for this assignment so that pod distribution across workers and ordered lifecycle behavior are observable; a single-node cluster would work for construction exercises but would hide the "StatefulSet pods spread across nodes by default" behavior that is part of the exam surface. kind's default `rancher.io/local-path` StorageClass satisfies `volumeClaimTemplates` without extra setup. See `docs/cluster-setup.md#multi-node-kind-cluster` for the cluster creation command and verification. No extra components (MetalLB, Calico, metrics-server, Gateway API CRDs) are required.

## Estimated Time Commitment

Plan for about 60 to 75 minutes on the tutorial; the tutorial builds a full StatefulSet, exercises its ordered lifecycle, performs a partitioned rolling update, and cleans up afterward, all while narrating the field defaults and failure modes. The 15 exercises together take four to six hours of focused work. Level 1 exercises run 10 to 15 minutes each once the StatefulSet mental model is in place. Level 2 exercises each take 20 to 30 minutes because they combine multiple concepts and because observing `OrderedReady` behavior or a partitioned rollout requires waiting for the controller to reconcile. Level 3 debugging exercises average 20 to 30 minutes because the failure modes are silent; the diagnostic work is the practice. Level 4 exercises run 30 to 45 minutes each: multi-tier applications require coordinating several manifests, and staged rollouts require stepping through partition boundaries. Level 5 exercises run 30 to 50 minutes each; the design task has no single right answer and the advanced debug scenario has multiple interacting problems.

## Scope Boundary and What Comes Next

This assignment covers the StatefulSet controller and its paired headless Service. It does not cover long-running stateless workloads (`Deployment`, `ReplicaSet`, `DaemonSet`), which live in `exercises/pods/assignment-7`. It does not cover batch workloads (`Job`, `CronJob`), which live in `exercises/jobs-and-cronjobs/assignment-1`. It does not cover the PV, PVC, and StorageClass mechanics that `volumeClaimTemplates` consumes; those are in the `exercises/storage/` series. It does not cover headless Service mechanics beyond the StatefulSet requirement; for broader Service material see `exercises/services/assignment-1`. Pod DNS resolution mechanics (how a pod DNS name becomes an IP) are covered in `exercises/coredns/assignment-1`. Horizontal autoscaling that can target StatefulSets is covered in `exercises/autoscaling/assignment-1`. Operator-based management of stateful workloads (PostgreSQL Operator, Redis Operator, and similar) is covered conceptually in `exercises/crds-and-operators/`. Backup and restore of stateful application data is out of CKA scope and is not treated here.

## Key Takeaways After Completing This Assignment

By the time you finish all 15 exercises, you should be able to decide quickly whether a workload belongs in a Deployment or a StatefulSet based on whether it needs sticky identity or sticky storage, and write the pair of objects (StatefulSet plus headless Service) together since neither is useful without the other. You should know the full StatefulSet spec surface well enough to write one from memory: `apiVersion: apps/v1`, `serviceName` pointing at a `clusterIP: None` Service, a `selector` that matches the template labels, a `template` with a pod spec, `replicas` (default 1), `volumeClaimTemplates` producing `<claim-name>-<statefulset-name>-<ordinal>` PVCs, `podManagementPolicy` (`OrderedReady` default or `Parallel`), and `updateStrategy` (`RollingUpdate` default with `partition` and `maxUnavailable`, or `OnDelete`). You should be able to resolve a specific pod by DNS name (`<pod>.<service>.<namespace>.svc.cluster.local`) from another pod in the cluster and understand why the Service must be headless for that to work. You should be able to verify that per-pod storage survives a pod restart, survives a reschedule, and survives a scale-down-then-scale-up cycle, and know that PVCs are deliberately not garbage-collected by the controller unless `persistentVolumeClaimRetentionPolicy` is configured. You should be able to execute a staged rollout using `partition`, understand that only pods with ordinal greater than or equal to the partition receive the new template, and recover a stuck rollout by rolling the template back and manually deleting the broken pod. Finally, you should be able to diagnose a StatefulSet in trouble by reading `kubectl describe statefulset`, `kubectl get pods -l <selector> -o wide`, pod Events, and ControllerRevision history, and know that an ImagePullBackOff on `pod-0` under `OrderedReady` blocks every subsequent pod until it recovers.
